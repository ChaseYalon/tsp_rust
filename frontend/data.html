Nicos Christophides at Imperial College London

That second algorithm there, Christofides' algorithm, was first proposed by Nicos Christofides. This must be one of the most influential algorithms on this list. It is fairly complicated, here is a link to the paper. The general idea is to first find the "Minimum Spanning Tree" (MST) and then convert that into an Eulerian circuit. Finally, it is transformed into a Hamiltonian cycle (a solution to the TSP).



Karl Menger from a 1970 issue of Shimer College Bulletin.

This is Karl Menger one of the origal creators of the Traveling Salsmen Problem. He wrote in 1933 about its suboptimality. It falls into the clasic phrase "the first solution is almost always wrong". Here that is certainly ture. The algorithm is as follows. Pick a random point to start, find the closest point and draw an edge between those two points. Repeat untill done. This is obviously simplistic and does not usually work very well (especialy on larger point sets).
